6 Ways to Cope With Financial Anxiety, From a Financial Therapist

Because money thoughts and feelings can be really overwhelming.

By Lindsay Bryan-Podvin, LMSW

April 6, 2022

Financial anxiety isn’t an official diagnosis, but many financial therapists (like myself) use the term to describe feeling nervous, worried, or on edge when engaging with money. While financial stress is usually related to an external trigger (“I lost my job because of downsizing; this is so stressful!”), financial anxiety tends to be more chronic and internal (“I’m constantly worried about losing my job; it’s one of my biggest fears.”). Financial anxiety happens to many of us, to some extent: In a 2021 study by the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority, more than 60% of the nearly 20,000 respondents said they experience anxiety when thinking about their personal finances. And, contrary to what you might think, financial anxiety can happen to anyone, regardless of how much money they earn or have in their bank account.

Much like other types of anxiety, financial anxiety tends to show up as excessive worry and fearful thoughts, which might include rehashing past money mistakes (“I can’t believe I put my couch on that credit card that charged 18% interest. I would have so much more money saved if I hadn’t made that mistake!”). Other examples include chronically worrying about future money moves (“Am I saving enough to be able to retire in 30 years?”) or being on edge about your current financial situation (“Am I paying too much in rent?”).

Anxiety is a warning sign from your nervous system that your mind and body perceive something as a potential threat. When you experience anxiety symptoms, your body is responding to that possible danger (whether it truly exists or not) by increasing hormones meant to help you fight or flee from whatever’s stressing you out (thanks, evolution!). The corresponding physiological response can lead to nausea, a racing heart, dizziness, or shortness of breath, among many other possible symptoms.

If this happens occasionally, it’s not necessarily a big deal. In fact, in the short term, your physiological stress response can help you focus on the here and now and allow you to meet the demands of stressful situations. Ongoing financial anxiety, on the other hand, isn’t so beneficial. While short-term stress can help you focus and complete tasks, consistently worrying about money can negatively impact your emotional and physical health: Chronic anxiety is linked with a slew of concerning physical symptoms, including potential digestive problems, depression, headaches, and trouble sleeping.

Most of us worry about financial issues like debt, job loss, and housing costs to some degree—and the economic uncertainty that grew in many households during the pandemic has made money matters more concerning for a lot of people—but how do you know if your financial anxiety is problematic? A general rule of thumb is to ask yourself, Is it preventing me from moving through the necessary tasks of my day? For example, does financial anxiety get in the way of you being able to look at your bank account or your budget and decide which areas of your spending are too high? Or does it keep you awake at night and leave you feeling exhausted and unfocused the next day?

When a client answers yes to that question, I know they need tools to help them manage their anxiety so it doesn’t take a serious toll on their well-being. If you can relate, these six strategies—that I recommend to all of my clients who experience financial anxiety—may help.

1. Figure out what, exactly, is triggering your financial anxiety.

First, it’s important to understand which financial tasks cause your anxiety. Is it day-to-day money management, like figuring out if you have enough money in your checking account before you go to lunch with friends, that makes your heart race? Or is it the big picture retirement and investment stuff that freaks you out? A common therapy-ism is “name it to tame it.” Identifying what causes anxiety is often the first step in managing it, and research has found that naming our feelings can help make them less intense. The idea behind this practice is that you activate the prefrontal region of your brain (which is generally responsible for higher-level cognitive functions, like planning and focusing) and this soothes the amygdala (that reptilian part of our brain responsible for fight or flight).

Here’s a (totally unrelated) example: If you had a child who was worried about a loud booming noise in the sky (amygdala triggered), you’d soothe them by saying something like, “It’s okay, that loud noise you hear is just thunder.” The same thinking can be applied to situations of intense financial stress or anxiety: Identifying that your panicky feeling is coming from opening your monthly credit card bill, say, may help your brain realize you’re not in immediate danger.

2. Challenge your mental money narrative. 

Another tool that can help you manage financial anxiety is to question anxious thoughts, which tend to spiral out of control. For example, you might think, I’m too embarrassed to ask for help with my 401k plan at work. That thought may then quickly spiral from “I’m embarrassed” to “Why even bother asking?” Instead, with kindness and curiosity, challenge your thinking by asking yourself, “How helpful is this thought? How true is it?” Then try applying it to the original problem. That might sound like, “Even though I’m a little embarrassed to ask about my retirement plan, I’m sure I’m not the only one with questions. I can handle feeling a little silly to make sure everything is correct.”

Another example of spiraling financial-anxiety thoughts could be jumping from having a poor credit score to never being able to own a home: “My credit score got dinged by 20 points. This is further proof that I’m bad with money. I’ll never be able to buy a house at this rate!” Instead, you could say to yourself, “Even though my credit score took a hit, I’ve learned that I can increase it over time. I’ll take steps to make sure I pay my credit cards on time, and I’m confident I can raise my credit score over the next year so I can apply for a mortgage and purchase a house.” Just like naming the source of your anxiety, changing your inner narrative from fear-based to fact-based can help your brain get unstuck from fight-or-flight mode.

3. Try body-based calming techniques.

Again, financial anxiety typically shows up physiologically, so tuning in and tending to your body’s sensations can sometimes help you relax. As I tell my clients, notice where your financial anxiety physically shows up and then offer yourself an antidote to the sensation. If your breaths are quick and shallow when looking at your bank account, for example, try the opposite by practicing deep, slow breaths. Box breathing is a calming breathing exercise where you inhale for a count of four, hold for a count of four, exhale for a count of four, and hold for another count of four. Try out a few rounds and see how your body feels.

Another tool I sometimes recommend when financial anxiety manifests physically is to, literally, shake it off. If your heart is racing, your stomach is in knots, or your breathing becomes shallow when thinking about signing up for life insurance at work, say, you can try standing up and shaking your arms or legs (I like to picture my overzealous Portuguese Water Dog Birdie doing one of her full-body shakes for inspiration). It might sound a little strange, but the technique is based on a therapy model developed by renowned trauma psychotherapist Peter Levine, PhD, called “somatic experiencing”—which involves processing and releasing tension and stress by tuning into and sometimes moving your body. Like other grounding techniques for anxiety, a bout of vigorous movement may help regulate your nervous system, at least in the moment.

4. Take your time learning about money.

Financial education is often preached as the way to end financial anxiety, and while it can certainly help, the mainstream advice to fill your brain with money management information is often short-sighted. Trying to learn everything you can about personal finance at once can cause you to feel overwhelmed, which, yep, can lead to financial anxiety! For example, someone might get very interested in zero-based budgeting (a method that accounts for every penny, every month), so they spend a lot of time researching the topic and creating the perfect color-coded spreadsheet, and then end up feeling like a failure when they overspend in a certain area, causing them to give up and decide that managing their money isn’t for them.

Instead, I recommend educating yourself in bite-sized chunks. Learn a bit about budgeting one month, for example, then listen to a podcast on emergency funds or try reading a book on investing the next month. I’m also a fan of regularly setting aside time to engage with money—I now schedule 45 minutes each month to dedicate to whatever financial to-dos are on my list, but when I was first getting started, it was 30 minutes per week. This way, when I experience a twinge of anxiety about a certain financial task, I know I have dedicated time to focus on it, which helps me feel calmer.

5. Come up with your own money language.

Jargon like “annuity,” “diversification,” “balance sheet,” and “time horizon” can be a turnoff in the money management space–especially if you’re experiencing financial anxiety. Many people I work with say they feel like personal finance isn’t for them because they’re intimidated by the cascade of confusing terms. Instead, try renaming terms with words that make you feel less anxious. For me, “budget” feels restrictive and punitive, so I use the term “spending plan” instead. The outcome is the same, but I’m less likely to feel anxious when using a term I feel more comfortable with. Other examples of this are calling an emergency fund a “life happens fund” or calling a retirement account a “financial freedom” account. This may seem trivial, but I’ve noticed (in myself and in my clients) that it can make engaging with money significantly more welcoming.

6. Know that it’s okay if you need to seek professional help.

While the above tips can certainly help dial down financial anxiety in certain circumstances, it’s important to note that they might not work for you—and it’s not your fault if they don’t. If your financial anxiety is ongoing and regularly impacts your ability to take care of yourself, get work done, and/or interact with others, a health care professional can help you determine the severity of your anxiety and offer treatment options to help you manage it. If you have one, you can ask your primary care provider for an anxiety assessment and potentially seek therapy, if you’re able. (The Financial Therapy Association has a database of therapists who specialize in financial wellness, some of which accept insurance and/or offer sliding-scale services.)

And if you’re struggling to make ends meet, are in a cycle of credit card debt, and/or are worried about your ability to pay for basic needs, you’ll also need more help than the advice in this article. In that case, working with a professional to figure out how to earn more and spend less so you can meet your financial needs is most important. If a financial therapist isn’t accessible to you, the United Way’s free programs on financial stability are a great resource—you can google “United Way financial stability + your county” to see if there’s help available in your area. Clever Girl Finance—an education platform focused on helping women achieve financial independence and closing the racial wealth gap—also offers a host of free courses that can support you in reaching your financial wellness goals.
